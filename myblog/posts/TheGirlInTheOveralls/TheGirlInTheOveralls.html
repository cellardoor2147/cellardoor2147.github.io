The Girl in the Overalls


3/10/2019




Before she said it, I thought we could be friends. “Why doesn’t Wesleyan use financial aid to pay for on-campus massages?”

I thought back to my ninth birthday. The park bench. Mom and Dad hurling expletives at someone - likely a bank teller - on the phone, no-doubt frustrated with defaulted loans and a lost house.

Then, I imagined this girl’s ninth birthday. A pool party in the backyard of a $300,000 estate. Goofy happy-go-lucky parents getting off early from their office jobs in order to pick up their daughter’s gift.

Would a massage have paid my parents’ debt and prevented our homelessness?

I wondered if her gift was a private masseuse.

Upon hearing that the topic of my rant would be low-income oppression, my peer, Katie, seemed disinterested. She didn’t seem concerned with the fact that another student had suggested the thread keeping Wesleyan affordable for me be replaced by a massage parlor.

I didn’t blame her. Beyond complaining, what had I ever done to combat the marginalization of the impoverished?

It was during this self-reflection that my phone vibrated. Monica Brinkley, my high school chemistry teacher, wanted me to speak over the Winter break to her students about the college application.

Upon arrival to her classroom, one thing was evident: rural Oklahoman students don’t come from money. Most of the class, Mrs. Brinkley included, had difficulty finding scholarships worth applying to; they cited a lack of some centralized hub as a challenge. I asked if they had looked into financial aid.

“What’s that,” a student asked.

I examined her clothing: ragged overalls overlaying a stained white shirt. In many ways both physical and abstract, her appearance was distinct from that of a Canada-Goose-jacket-wearing Wesleyan student. How could she be expected to understand a process that was meant to filter her out?

“It’s how I afford college,” I replied.

Given this situation, Katie readily agreed to act as a graphic designer and contributing writer to my proposition: the CAF project. The College Application Fairness project is not designed to spite Wesleyan’s elitist populous. Rather, it’s meant to prevent ignorant massage-loving complacence from impeding the success of the girl in overalls.
